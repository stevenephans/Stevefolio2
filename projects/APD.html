Advanced Product Design

Product Design, CAD, 3D-Printing, Laser Cutting, Sewing

Advanced Product Design is the first of four required courses in CU's design track for graduate mechanical engineering students. The class teaches the Design Thinking approach to product development. Each group determines a problem affecting the community and spends the semester thoughtfully building up a solution based-on the needs of the user.

We chose dog poop....more specifically, pet waste left uncleaned in public spaces. It's an unpleasant addition to the community and an underratedly insidious detriment to the environment.

Phase 1: The Root of the Problem

Design Thinking is a human-centered approach to product development. Before any ideation took place, we needed to understand our user's point-of-view. Why don't they always pick up after their pets? What's the worst bit about the clean-up process? We interviewed numerous dog owners in the Boulder area, listening to their views on the issue and looking for hidden insights and common themes.


We extracted two main, addressable points from these initial interviews:
1) If there's a trashcan nearby, clean-up is no big deal. But having to carry a loaded bag around indefinitely is awful.
2) Dog waste is no fun to handle or smell (big surprise there!). The less direct contact, the better.

We drafted the following 'need statement' from our observations:
Dog owners need an easy, reliable, and comfortable way to deal with dog waste until they can dispose of it properly.

Phase 2: 'Pretotype' Development

With these broad-stroke needs of our users in mind, our team began brainstorming every possible solution we could imagine. We charted these ideas in the concept map below.

After reviewing our ideas, we felt a portable receptacle, optimized for ease of use and robust waste containment, would best address our user's needs. We synthesized our ideas into three unique 'pretotype' designs for the first round of user testing: a rigid bodied container designed to serve as a leash handle replacement, a flexible container with a multipurpose clip for storage/attachment, and a variable size waterproof roll-top bag.

'Pretotypes' are a sort of quick and dirty prototype. They are intentionally low-fidelity, as their primary purpose is to embody the fundamental characteristics of an idea; they help determine if users think an idea is worthy of further pursuit.


We were happy to find that all our users interviewed found the idea of a dedicated, on-the-go waste container very appealing! Observing them interact with the pretotypes, and listening to their thoughts, we established desired features for the next stage of development.

Users wanted a rigid bodied container with sufficient space for 2-3 waste bags, but not so large as to impinge on convenient portability. Ideally the lid could be operated with a single hand and would stay reliably sealed. Many users liked the 'leash handle' attachment idea, but pointed out that it was not a viable option for all leash styles. We decided if it could be included, great, but a design that fit in a backpack (human or dog) with a multipurpose attachment point would provide a more universally appealing solution.

Phase 3: Initial Prototypes

Our first round of prototypes addressed the needs gleaned from the pretotype studies while further optimizing for ease of use. We fabricated a lid mechanism designed for one-handed operation, two unique main-body models to evaluate ergonomics as well as waste capacity and device stowability, and finally a novel bag securement technology that would allow for speedy and convenient storage and removal of filled waste bags.

The lid prototype was well received during user testing. It functioned smoothly and reliably with only one hand and remained sealed when dropped from heights of up to 6ft.



Both main-body prototypes felt good in hand and fit nicely in all the backpacks we tried. Each featured a flared mouth for easy bag placement which tapered down to a slightly reduced diameter (2.5in and 3.5in). User's agreed the smaller of the two didn't provide adequate room for effortless waste bag input, so we opted for the larger option moving forward.



During pretotype testing, we observed users still had to knot the bags before they could safely store them. Tying knots is an unfortunately two-handed endeavor, and having to do so undermined our ability to create a purely one-handed waste management experience. Determined to solve the issue, we developed a system of rubber-lined notches. These 'No-Knot-Notches' effectively sealed and secured the used bags, eliminating the need for knot tying! The exposed bag end also provided a convenient access point when removing bags from the container. Users were thrilled with the innovation!

Phase 4: The Final Prototype

After a semester of user research, testing, and development, we synthesized all we learned into a 'final prototype' for extended user testing. Our lucky chosen user, Ben, took it to the streets and ran it through the gauntlet of real-world, day-to-day use.
